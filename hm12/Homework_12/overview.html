Colombia and its conflict

Colombia has long been affected by guerrilla violence and government military actions in conjunction with 30 years of government efforts for the promotion of dialogue with guerrilla groups to achieve peace. The ownership of most land by less than 5 percent of the population has been a constant source of conflict in Colombia, manifested for example in the ongoing struggle to expand the agricultural frontier. The persistence of the Colombian conflict has left a deep wound in the social structures. This work presents an overview.

Investment

Historically, government expenditures on Research and Development (R&D) has been negligible compared to the size of the economy. The Observatory of Science and Technology of Colombia reported in 2015 that the proportion of R&D investment between the government and the private sector had decreased from a ratio of 1:2 to 1:5 between 2005 and 2015.
Also, the beginning of the 2010s was met with another major investment in military defense in a country facing increased violence and forced displacement

Expeding

By the beggining of 1990s the country faced an escalation of the conflict with guerrillas, who had become more violent and started practices of terrorism, kidnapping, forced displacements, and attacks on public infrastructure.
For example, guerrilla groups orchestrated attacks on nationally regulated oil pipelines so as to limit money entering local and federal economy.
Also guerrillas were known by the use of land mines as a medium to debilitate military actions




Colombia has experienced a major and prolonged guerrilla conflict that has had a major impact on the development and progress of the country. Over the last twenty years, multiple actions such as modernizing the state or increasing military expenditures have not addressed the lack of development and perpetuation of poverty in the rural areas

Victims

The conflict has left millions of victims over the last 50 years.
